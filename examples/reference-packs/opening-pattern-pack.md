# Opening Pattern Pack

This pack is a teaching surface, not a law.
Use it to compare what an opening is *doing*, not just how flashy it looks.

All fragments below are synthetic and original.

## O-01. Opening Job: Immediate Conflict

**When to use**  
When the opening needs to throw the viewer into active pressure quickly because the story is powered by pursuit, escape, public exposure, or live threat.

**Creative problem**  
The script wants urgency, but the opening keeps spending time introducing the world before the danger is felt.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. DISTRICT COURT HALLWAY - MORNING

LINA rounds the corner and sees her own photograph taped outside Courtroom 3.
Below it: WITNESS REFUSES TO APPEAR.

Her lawyer is already running toward her.

LAWYER
If you walk in now, they will ask why your statement changed.

LINA
If I do not walk in, they will say I was bought.
```

**Why this works**  
The opening immediately establishes public risk, a live deadline, and a choice with two bad outcomes. It is not “busy”; it is doing a job.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. COURTHOUSE - MORNING

LINA walks through the courthouse and looks nervous.
People pass by.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It describes mood but withholds the actual pressure source. The scene wants urgency without naming what is urgent.

**Non-dogma note**  
Not every opening needs this much pressure. The point is not speed by default. The point is matching the opening job to the real engine.

## O-02. Opening Job: Question Gap

**When to use**  
When curiosity is the main entry point and the story benefits from a clear, concrete missing piece.

**Creative problem**  
The script wants intrigue, but the opening only generates vagueness.

**Strong fragment**
```text
EXT. SCHOOL ROOFTOP - DAWN

On the ledge sits a paper crane with a student ID tied to its neck.

The ID belongs to a girl who transferred out six months ago.

The janitor picks up the crane and finds fresh ash inside the folded wings.
```

**Why this works**  
The question gap is specific. Who placed it? Why now? Why ash? The opening gives the viewer something concrete to pursue.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
EXT. SCHOOL ROOFTOP - DAWN

Something feels strange about the morning.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
“Strange” is mood, not a question. The audience is told to be curious without being given a real object of curiosity.

**Non-dogma note**  
Question-gap openings still need downstream payoff. A mysterious image without a structural follow-through becomes decorative suspense.

## O-03. Opening Job: Game / Premise Promise

**When to use**  
When the project lives or dies on a repeatable rule, premise engine, or central social game.

**Creative problem**  
The pilot or short-form opening needs to show what kind of story machine this is, not just who the lead is.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. NIGHT DELIVERY HUB - 02:11 A.M.

Every undelivered parcel on the wall flashes red after midnight.

MING checks the screen.
Three failed deliveries means suspension.
One missed parcel means a customer complaint.
One opened parcel means criminal liability.

Her mentor slides a box toward her.

MENTOR
Choose which problem you want. You do not get none.
```

**Why this works**  
The opening establishes the governing rule set and the repeatable conflict engine. We understand the series machine before exposition arrives.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. DELIVERY HUB - NIGHT

MING starts a hard new job and hopes it goes well.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It introduces a circumstance but not the system that will generate story pressure.

**Non-dogma note**  
A premise-promise opening can still be quiet. What matters is that the central game becomes legible.

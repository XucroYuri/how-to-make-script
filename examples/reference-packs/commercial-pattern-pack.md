# Commercial Pattern Pack

This pack is synthetic and callable. It is meant to help an agent recognize commercial screenplay situations, not to act like a canon or a style bible.

## Classification Axes

- `message_shape`: one promise, one tension, one proof chain, or one atmosphere.
- `brand_visibility`: front-loaded, integrated, or delayed.
- `conversion_goal`: awareness, recall, click, trial, store visit, or purchase.
- `proof_mode`: demo, contrast, testimonial, metaphor, or behavior.
- `platform_pressure`: TV, vertical shortform, branded film, or mixed placement.
- `friction_level`: simple product, complex product, premium identity, or trust barrier.

## Scenario C01: One Promise, One Action

When to use: The product is simple, the use case is obvious, and the spot must land fast.

Creative problem: make one benefit feel inevitable without stacking three other benefits on top of it.

Strong fragment:

```text
VO: "You do not need a new routine. You need a cleaner first step."
SHOT 1: A hand drops the old bottle into the trash.
SHOT 2: Water runs. The new bottle appears once, then stays out of the way.
SUPER: "Start lighter."
```

Why this works: the message is singular, the action is legible, and the brand does not compete with the promise.

Weaker fragment:

```text
VO: "Modern. Smart. Premium. Eco-friendly. Built for every day."
SHOT 1: Product spin.
SHOT 2: Logo lockup.
SHOT 3: App screen.
```

Why the weaker version fails: it enumerates qualities instead of creating one decisive reason to care.

Non-dogma note: a more poetic or slower route can work if the platform rewards mood over clarity.

## Scenario C02: Brand Film With Human Value

When to use: The brief asks for emotional lift, reputation building, or a brand story that needs memory rather than direct conversion.

Creative problem: make the brand feel earned through human behavior instead of announcing values as slogans.

Strong fragment:

```text
VO: "The city does not get quieter. We just learn who still needs us after dark."
SHOT 1: A technician leaves a repair van.
SHOT 2: A baker reopens a warm storefront.
SHOT 3: A teenager calls home from a lit bus stop.
END CARD: Brand mark appears after the human sequence.
```

Why this works: the brand sits inside a value proposition that is dramatized through observed behavior, not stated as a claim.

Weaker fragment:

```text
VO: "We care. We are committed. We stand for progress."
SHOT 1: Slow-motion logo.
SHOT 2: Corporate handshake.
SHOT 3: Abstract skyline.
```

Why the weaker version fails: it describes virtue without showing a recognizable human consequence.

Non-dogma note: some brand films should be quieter or stranger; the rule is not "show action at all costs", but "earn the value."

## Scenario C03: Vertical Hook To Proof

When to use: The first two seconds must stop scroll, and the proof must arrive before attention decays.

Creative problem: turn a claim into a visible reversal or a small surprise.

Strong fragment:

```text
ON SCREEN: "I thought this would take all morning."
CUTS: A tap, a wipe, a reset, done.
ON SCREEN: "It took 12 seconds."
VO: "That is the point."
```

Why this works: it opens with a friction-based confession, then resolves it with visible proof.

Weaker fragment:

```text
VO: "Here is an amazing new solution for busy people."
CUT TO: smiling user.
CUT TO: logo.
CTA: "Learn more."
```

Why the weaker version fails: the hook is generic, so the viewer has no reason to keep watching long enough for the proof.

Non-dogma note: some shortform ads are better when they begin with a scene, not a claim, if the brand can still be recognized quickly.

## Scenario C04: Objection Handling Ad

When to use: the buyer already has resistance, skepticism, or comparison anxiety.

Creative problem: answer the objection without sounding defensive or policy-heavy.

Strong fragment:

```text
VO: "Yes, it costs more than the bargain option."
SHOT 1: Split screen of one failed cheap fix and one clean install.
VO: "That is because the cheap option asked you to buy twice."
END LINE: "Pay once. Move on."
```

Why this works: the script names the objection and turns it into a concrete tradeoff.

Weaker fragment:

```text
VO: "Our quality is unmatched. Our value is exceptional. Our customers love us."
SHOT 1: Product glamour.
SHOT 2: Happy faces.
SHOT 3: Claim stack.
```

Why the weaker version fails: it dodges the objection instead of metabolizing it.

Non-dogma note: not every objection should be answered directly; some are better handled by demonstration or comparison without calling out the objection textually.

## Scenario C05: Category Education

When to use: the audience does not yet understand what the product category is, why it exists, or why it matters.

Creative problem: teach the category without making the ad feel like a lecture.

Strong fragment:

```text
VO: "This is not another cleaner. It is a 30-second reset for the room you actually live in."
SHOT 1: Messy kitchen.
SHOT 2: Fast, local cleaning action.
SHOT 3: Room returns to normal use.
SUPER: "Category: reset, not polish."
```

Why this works: it frames the category in a human use model, not a technical taxonomy.

Weaker fragment:

```text
VO: "Introducing a next-generation multi-surface enzymatic system."
SHOT 1: Abstract particles.
SHOT 2: Lab visuals.
SHOT 3: Feature list.
```

Why the weaker version fails: it explains internals before the audience knows why they matter.

Non-dogma note: a technical audience may want more system detail; the educational burden depends on the room.

## Scenario C06: Premium Mood Piece

When to use: the product sells identity, taste, or aspiration, and direct explanation would flatten the appeal.

Creative problem: make the audience feel the status of the product before they can summarize it.

Strong fragment:

```text
SHOT 1: A late train, empty platform, clean coat, quiet breath.
SHOT 2: The product appears only in action, not in hero pose.
VO: "Some purchases are not about more. They are about less noise."
END CARD: Logo, minimal line.
```

Why this works: the script builds a sensory and social mood that makes the brand position feel lived, not argued.

Weaker fragment:

```text
VO: "Luxury for the modern achiever."
SHOT 1: Shiny object montage.
SHOT 2: Generic skyline.
SHOT 3: Gold typography.
```

Why the weaker version fails: it relies on prestige words instead of prestige behavior.

Non-dogma note: premium pieces can still be funny, intimate, or messy; polish is not the same thing as distance.

## Scenario C07: Seasonal Urgency Without Panic

When to use: the campaign needs a deadline, but the tone should stay trustworthy.

Creative problem: create urgency without shouting.

Strong fragment:

```text
VO: "The first cold week is not the time to discover your old habit no longer fits."
SHOT 1: One person in a thin jacket hesitates.
SHOT 2: The new product solves the problem quickly.
SHOT 3: The second morning feels easier.
```

Why this works: it ties urgency to a concrete lived moment instead of abstract scarcity.

Weaker fragment:

```text
VO: "Act now. Limited time. Biggest deal ever. Last chance."
SHOT 1: Flashing text.
SHOT 2: Countdown.
SHOT 3: Loud CTA.
```

Why the weaker version fails: pressure replaces relevance, so the viewer feels pushed rather than persuaded.

Non-dogma note: some promotions need hard urgency; the point is to match urgency style to trust level.

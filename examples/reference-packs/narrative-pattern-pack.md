# Narrative Pattern Pack

This pack is a reference library, not a canon.
Use it to compare successful narrative moves across common screenplay scenarios, then decide whether the current task should follow, adapt, or reject the pattern.

All fragments below are synthetic and original. They are written to show structure, pressure, payoff, and failure modes, not to imitate existing films or shows.

## N-01. Feature Film: Guilt-Centered Return

**When to use**  
When the story begins with a character returning to a place they once fled, and the return itself needs to carry plot, theme, and emotional consequence.

**Creative problem**  
The script needs a reason the homecoming cannot stay sentimental. The return must reopen a wound and force a choice.

**Strong fragment**
```text
EXT. FLOOD-DAMAGED MAIN ROAD - DUSK

MARA steps off the bus with a cardboard suitcase and sees the town sign half-swallowed by mud.

ON HER PHONE: a voice note from her brother.
BROTHER (V.O.)
If you come back, come before the council meeting. They found the ledger.

Mara looks at the courthouse across the square. Then at the old clinic where her father used to work.

MARA
(to herself)
So it starts here again.
```

**Why this works**  
The return is not just setting. It activates a concealed record, a public deadline, and a personal debt in the same beat. The scene also gives the protagonist a choice point, which is what turns location into drama.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
EXT. SMALL TOWN - DUSK

MARA arrives home. She looks around sadly.

MARA
I have not been here in years.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It states the premise but does not pressure it. There is no concrete external trigger, no competing obligation, and no reason the return cannot simply be a mood beat.

**Non-dogma note**  
Some return stories work as quiet observation pieces. They do not need a ledger or deadline, but they still need a pressure source that prevents the homecoming from becoming pure atmosphere.

## N-02. Feature Film: Moral Complicity Reveal

**When to use**  
When the story’s engine depends on the protagonist discovering that they helped create the problem they are now trying to solve.

**Creative problem**  
The twist must reframe prior action without feeling like a cheap reversal.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. ARCHIVE ROOM - NIGHT

The files on the table are older than the city logo stamped on them.

JON opens a folder marked BRIDGE FIRE REPORT.
Inside is his signature.

JON
No.

LEENA
You signed the version that buried the evacuation delay.

JON
I signed the one they gave me.

LEENA
That is still your signature.
```

**Why this works**  
The reveal is specific, document-based, and morally uncomfortable. The scene shifts the protagonist from investigator to implicated participant, which adds a deeper layer of conflict than “someone hid the truth.”

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. OFFICE - NIGHT

JON finds out the company did something bad long ago.

JON
I never knew.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It offloads responsibility too cleanly. The protagonist remains morally separate from the problem, so the story loses self-incrimination and becomes a generic expose.

**Non-dogma note**  
Not every investigation needs self-implication. Some stories work best when the protagonist is an outsider. This pattern is strongest when guilt is part of the theme, not when the story simply needs a cleaner villain.

## N-03. Episodic Pilot: Repeatable Story Engine

**When to use**  
When the project is episodic and the pilot must prove the series can generate fresh stories after episode one.

**Creative problem**  
The pilot cannot feel like a finished film in disguise. It must establish a repeatable engine.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. NIGHT SHIFT DISPATCH - 01:12 A.M.

Every ring on the emergency line becomes a new category of problem.

NIA scans three calls at once.

NIA
One missing person, one tenant lockout, one false alarm. Which one gets me fired tomorrow?

The supervisor points at the board.

SUPERVISOR
The one that gets solved without you inventing a new rule.
```

**Why this works**  
The pilot defines the series engine as a weekly collision between urgent cases and an institutional rule set. The premise is reusable, and the scene promises future variation without needing to explain all future episodes.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. DISPATCH - NIGHT

NIA has a bad day at work and learns to be a better person.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It describes an arc but not a system. Without a repeatable conflict model, the pilot may work as a one-off but cannot support episode generation.

**Non-dogma note**  
Some limited-series pilots can be more self-contained. The engine still matters, but it can be narrower if the show is intentionally finite.

## N-04. Episodic Story: B-Plot That Changes the A-Plot

**When to use**  
When an episodic script needs the secondary storyline to actually alter the main storyline, not just decorate it.

**Creative problem**  
The B-plot often floats beside the A-plot instead of pressuring it.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. COMMUNITY KITCHEN - AFTERNOON

While ELISE negotiates with the landlord about the rent hike, her younger brother rearranges the storage shelves by emergency priority.

ELISE
Why are the canned goods stacked by the door?

BROTHER
Because if we lose power, soup matters more than symmetry.

The landlord notices the new layout and asks what kind of place this is becoming.
Elise hears the question as a threat.
```

**Why this works**  
The B-plot action changes the way the main conflict is perceived. It is not a separate lesson. It directly reframes the room, the power dynamics, and the decision being negotiated.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. COMMUNITY KITCHEN - AFTERNOON

ELISE argues about rent.

CUT TO: her brother organizing labels in the pantry.

ELISE later finishes the argument.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
The B-plot is a cutaway, not a pressure source. It has no causal effect on the A-plot, so it can be removed without changing the episode.

**Non-dogma note**  
Some episodes intentionally use a light B-plot for tone or pacing. That can work, but then the script should be honest about its function instead of pretending it is structurally consequential.

## N-05. Short Drama: Hook Density Under Compression

**When to use**  
When the format is short drama or vertical serial content and each beat needs to earn attention quickly.

**Creative problem**  
The script must establish relationship, conflict, and a turn before the viewer drops.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. TINY APARTMENT - MORNING

The landlord is already inside, photographing the walls.

YI-LAN freezes in the doorway.

LANDLORD
I am documenting the leak.

YI-LAN
That is my wedding wall.

The landlord lowers the phone.

LANDLORD
Then it is leaking in a more expensive way than I expected.
```

**Why this works**  
The scene introduces invasion, property threat, and a personal emotional stake in a few lines. The last line turns the conflict and keeps the hook alive.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. APARTMENT - MORNING

YI-LAN wakes up. She looks at the wall. She remembers the past.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It burns time on internal mood without creating an immediate external collision. Short-form drama cannot afford to stay abstract that long.

**Non-dogma note**  
Short drama is not identical to action-heavy writing. A slow-burn short can still work if every beat reveals a new relational pressure, not just a new image.

## N-06. Animation: Visual Idea With Emotional Legibility

**When to use**  
When the story is for animation and the visual world can carry metaphor, but the emotional logic still has to read cleanly.

**Creative problem**  
The script should exploit animation-specific imagery without becoming decorative or confusing.

**Strong fragment**
```text
EXT. CITY OF BELL TOWERS - SUNRISE

Every bell that rings makes the pigeons lift in a different pattern.

MIO, 12, keeps her own shadow in a jar so it will not run ahead of her.

MIO
If it gets there first, it will tell on me.

The shadow bumps the glass from inside.
```

**Why this works**  
The visual rule is clear, the metaphor is active, and the child logic is understandable. The world is imaginative but still attached to a simple emotional need: fear of being exposed.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
EXT. FANTASY CITY - SUNRISE

The city is magical and beautiful.

MIO has a problem with identity.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It states vibe and theme without giving the animation anything to stage. There is no specific visual contract and no sceneable emotional object.

**Non-dogma note**  
Animation does not require surrealism. A grounded animated drama can still succeed if the visuals are precise and the emotional logic is readable.

## N-07. Mystery / Thriller: Clue Economy

**When to use**  
When the script needs suspense, but the audience should feel the clues were fair rather than arbitrary.

**Creative problem**  
The writer must hide information without cheating the viewer.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. LAUNDROMAT - LATE EVENING

The spinning machine keeps stopping on the same red sock.

MARA checks the lint trap and pulls out a strip of receipt paper.

On it: a number, a time, and the word WEST.

The owner of the laundromat sees her reading it.

OWNER
That was not in the pockets when she came in.
```

**Why this works**  
The clue is embedded in an ordinary action and has enough specificity to be trackable later. The scene also introduces a witness who can complicate the clue’s meaning.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. LAUNDROMAT - LATE EVENING

MARA finds a mysterious clue.

MARA
This changes everything.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It withholds the clue’s nature and function. Suspense depends on calibrated reveal, not on mystery as an empty label.

**Non-dogma note**  
Some mysteries deliberately obscure the clue path for dream logic or unreliable narration. That can work, but then the script must replace fair-play clueing with a different audience contract.

## N-08. Rom-Com: Desire Mismatch With Comic Pressure

**When to use**  
When the story needs attraction and conflict to coexist in the same scene.

**Creative problem**  
The scene must be funny, but the comedy should grow out of incompatible desires, not just wisecracks.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. BAKERY AFTER CLOSE - NIGHT

TESS is labeling boxes for a charity event. OWEN is trying to rescue a half-burnt cake.

TESS
Why are you still here?

OWEN
Because your volunteers left me alone with dessert and a moral injury.

TESS
That is not a category.

OWEN
It is when you say "low sugar" and mean "I am judging you spiritually."
```

**Why this works**  
The characters want different things from the moment, and the conflict is already romantic-comic because the banter is tied to behavior and judgment. The scene gives attraction room to emerge through friction.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. BAKERY - NIGHT

TESS and OWEN talk a lot and have chemistry.

They laugh.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
Chemistry is asserted instead of dramatized. The scene does not expose incompatible aims or pressure the relationship into motion.

**Non-dogma note**  
Romantic stories can also work through stillness, restraint, or shared silence. The key is not joke density. It is the pressure between what each character wants from the interaction.

## N-09. Ensemble / Workplace Drama: Colliding Agendas

**When to use**  
When multiple characters must feel distinct and the scene should produce conflict without relying on a single villain.

**Creative problem**  
The dialogue must not flatten everyone into the same opinion.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. SCHOOL AUDITORIUM - DAY

The fundraiser budget sits on the projector.

PRINCIPAL
We either cut the play or cut the buses.

TEACHER
The play is the reason half the students still come after lunch.

TREASURER
The buses are the reason they can get here at all.

STUDENT LEAD
If you cancel the play, you also cancel the one thing we built that was not already broken.
```

**Why this works**  
Each speaker represents a different value system. The scene gains energy because the conflict is not personal abuse; it is competing priorities under real constraint.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. SCHOOL AUDITORIUM - DAY

Everyone agrees the budget is bad.
They all worry about the school.
They all want the same solution.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
If everyone shares the same position, there is no ensemble tension. The scene becomes summary rather than drama.

**Non-dogma note**  
Not every workplace scene needs open argument. Some can work through quiet alignment, irony, or withheld disagreement. But if the scene is meant to carry ensemble pressure, the agendas need to diverge.

## N-10. Long-Form Rewrite: Make the Scene Earn Its Place

**When to use**  
When revising a draft that has scenes, beats, or dialogue that feel competent but unnecessary.

**Creative problem**  
The script has material that is fine in isolation but weak in sequence.

**Strong fragment**
```text
INT. HOSPITAL CORRIDOR - NIGHT

The doctor hands REN a visitor badge.

DOCTOR
You can see him for ten minutes.

REN
I did not come for a visit.

DOCTOR
Then do not waste this one.
```

**Why this works**  
The exchange carries deadline, resistance, and thematic pressure in a compact form. It advances the emotional problem rather than merely explaining the situation.

**Weaker contrasting fragment**
```text
INT. HOSPITAL CORRIDOR - NIGHT

REN walks slowly.

REN
This is hard.

She keeps walking.
```

**Why the weaker version fails**  
It is tonal but not functional. The scene does not alter the story state, so it may feel pretty while contributing little.

**Non-dogma note**  
Not every good scene must be high-pressure. Some scenes exist to breathe, but even breathing scenes need a deliberate role in rhythm, perspective, or release.

## How To Use This Pack

- Use the strong fragment as a pattern reference, not as a template to copy.
- Treat the weaker fragment as a failure mode, not as an absolute prohibition.
- If a different route better fits the medium, audience, or production context, take it.
- The goal is to make successful shapes visible, not to claim they are the only successful shapes.
